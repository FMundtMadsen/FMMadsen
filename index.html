About

I am a molecular biologist with nearly two decades of experience studying how life works at the molecular level.

I received my PhD in medical science from the Karolinska Institute and have conducted research at institutions including the Broad Institute of MIT and Harvard, and the University of Copenhagen. My work has focused on molecular and cellular biology, with applications ranging from cancer and diabetes to emerging diseases.

I currently work in translational medicine, where the goal is to connect molecular understanding to real-world biology and human health.

Alongside research, I have spent many years teaching molecular biology and communicating science to broader audiences. Over time, this has led to a simple realization:

Biology is not as complicated as it first appears.

What This Is About

We tend to think of life as overwhelmingly complex.

Trillions of cells. Billions of interactions. Constant motion.

But when we look closely, much of this complexity follows a small set of simple physical rules:

  • Opposite charges attract
  • Like charges repel
  • Two things cannot occupy the same space

These rules govern how molecules behave inside every cell.

From these simple constraints emerge chains of interactions—one event triggering the next.

Like dominoes.

This is the perspective I explore here:

How simple rules give rise to the complexity of life.

Newsletter

These essays are written for a general audience, but grounded in real science.

They follow a simple idea:
